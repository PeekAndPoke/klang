# Motör slogan state (as of 2026-04-11)

## Decided / leading

- **"Sound First!"** — the user-facing promise. What the listener gets. Remains the primary slogan.
- **"Come closer."** — the invitation slogan, paired with "Sound First!". My recommended secondary. Drops "to the
  machine" because the engine metaphor is already carried by the entire product surface (Cylinder, Injection, Ignitor,
  Katalyst). Two words, mysterious, conspiratorial, action-verb agency, leaves the listener wanting to peek.
- **"Come closer to the machine."** — fallback secondary if the explicit machine tribute needs to stay load-bearing.
  Still a clear win over the original and over the "Be" variant.

## Ruled out (and why)

- **"Closer to the machine"** — too inward. A producer's manifesto, no addressee, no "you" in the room. Machine-worship
  without an invitation. This was the original tension that prompted the rework.
- **"Be closer to the machine"** — half-measure. "Be" is a state verb — it asks the listener to *already exist* in the
  relationship instead of crossing into it. Grammatically dangling ("closer than what?"). Loses the agency and movement
  that "come" carries. Research on persuasive slogans consistently shows action verbs outperform state verbs (see "Just
  Do It" vs the hypothetical "Just Be Doing It").
- **"Prepare, Ignite, Refine"** — same machine-centric problem as "Closer to the machine". Producer-inward, no listener.

## Why the invitation framing works

The original slogans failed not because of *what* they said but *who they addressed* — nobody. Adding an invitation
verb ("come") puts a "you" in the room without surrendering the machine metaphor. The machine stays center stage, but
now there's a hand extended toward someone standing at the door. This is the move that resolves the tension between
machine-worship (which is the brand's soul) and user-orientation (which is what makes a slogan land).

## Why "Come" beats "Be"

1. "Come" implies movement — the listener is currently outside, which is honest about most people's relationship to the
   engine metaphor.
2. "Come" implies the machine is worth approaching — curiosity hook.
3. "Come closer" is the verb of intimacy — what you say before sharing a secret. Conspiratorial, warm, draws people in.
4. Action verbs carry grammatical agency; state verbs don't.

## Open question

Whether to commit to the bare "Come closer." or keep "Come closer to the machine." User preference depends on how
load-bearing the explicit machine tribute is in their heart.
